\section{Static baselines: exposure and mobility}\label{sec:baseline}

Before adding any dynamics, we ask how far purely \emph{static}, single-crystal descriptors already
get --- both to set the bar the dynamics features must clear and to make the proximity critique of
\S\ref{sec:motivation} quantitative on our own labels. We test the two classic static proxies for
``epitope-ness'': surface \textbf{exposure} (this section) and \textbf{mobility} (crystallographic
B-factors, below). The exposure descriptor is per-residue \textbf{relative solvent-accessible
surface area} (rel.\ SASA): Shrake--Rupley SASA on the input crystal (antigen chain only), normalised
by the residue-type maximum~\cite{tien2013}. This is a single-frame calculation on the input crystal
structures --- the pre-trajectory baseline; once the production runs land it is replaced by
trajectory-averaged SASA at the same residue keys (and joined by RMSF, non-bonded energetics, and
hydration).

\paragraph{Which structures (a confound shared by both baselines).} Both descriptors are read from the
deposited crystal of each antigen, and for \emph{six of the eight} the crystal is the antibody--antigen
\textbf{complex} --- only lysozyme (\texttt{1AKI}) and hGH (\texttt{1HGU}) are true apo structures. This
biases the two features differently. For SASA we compute on the antigen chain \emph{alone} (antibody
atoms deleted), so the epitope surface is not buried in the calculation --- but the coordinates are the
\emph{bound} conformation, so these are holo-state SASA values, equal to apo only absent induced fit.
For B-factors the bias is more direct: the antibody is present in the crystal and damps exactly the
epitope residues' B-factors, which on its own could \emph{manufacture} the rigid-hotspot signal below.
The two genuine apo structures trend the same as the rest (B-factor $\rho=-0.23,\,-0.40$), so the
patterns are not pure artefacts --- but a single crystal, especially a complex, cannot cleanly report
the \emph{free} antigen. Apo-MD removes this at the root: trajectory SASA and RMSF are computed on the
unbound antigen, no antibody and no lattice. We therefore read the static baselines as motivating
bounds, not clean measurements.

\paragraph{Setup.} We pool every residue of the eight $\ddG$ antigens ($1{,}790$ residues).
A residue is \emph{important} if its alanine-scan $\ddG \geq 1$\,kcal/mol for any antibody
(aggregated as the max over antibodies). An exposure-only predictor labels a residue an epitope
if rel.\ SASA $\geq 0.25$ (the standard ``exposed'' cutoff) --- the static analogue of a proximity
label. We then read off precision and recall against the experimental importance labels.

\paragraph{Result.} Exposure behaves exactly as the thesis predicts. It has \emph{moderate recall}
(it knows roughly where the surface is: $63\%$ of importance hotspots are exposed) but
\emph{near-zero precision} ($\approx 3\%$): rel.\ SASA $\geq 0.25$ sweeps in almost half the protein,
overwhelmingly residues that contribute no binding energy. Per antigen the precision is $0$--$14\%$
(highest for the clean single-domain antigens \texttt{1AKI}, \texttt{1BJ1}, \texttt{1JRH}). And
\emph{among the scanned interface residues}, exposure does not even rank graded importance: the
pooled Spearman correlation between rel.\ SASA and $\ddG$ is $-0.16$ --- once a residue is on the
surface, how exposed it is says nothing about how much binding energy it carries. That gap ---
high recall, no precision, no within-interface ranking --- is precisely what the dynamic and
energetic features are meant to fill, and every feature we add is scored against this baseline.

\paragraph{Caveats.} (i) Single-frame apo SASA; trajectory-averaging will move values and is the
real control. (ii) \texttt{5C6T} (gB) is anomalous here --- it is scored on the unrepaired structure
(\S\ref{sec:repair}) \emph{and} as an isolated protomer of a native trimer, so residues buried in the
trimer interface read as exposed; \texttt{2JEL} (HPr) carries only one hotspot. (iii) Residues never
scanned are treated as non-epitope, which alanine scans largely justify (they target the known
epitope) but which inflates the negative set.

\paragraph{Static mobility (crystallographic B-factors) --- the dynamics teaser.} A second,
long-standing idea links antigenicity to \emph{flexibility}: mobile surface regions are more
antigenic~\cite{westhof1984}. The static, experimental proxy for residue mobility is the
crystallographic B-factor, and it is the \emph{static analogue of the RMSF} we will extract from the
apo trajectories --- so a B-factor signal would foreshadow the dynamics payoff. We test it the same
way: per-residue C$\alpha$ B-factor, z-normalised per structure (raw B-factors are incomparable
across crystals --- resolution, refinement, overall scaling), against $\ddG$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:bfactor-baseline}). The result runs \emph{opposite} to the naive expectation. The
energetic hotspots are among the most \emph{rigid} residues, not the most mobile: pooled Spearman
$\rho = -0.29$, negative in six of eight antigens, with whole-protein AUC $<0.5$ (anti-discriminative).
Residues that pay the most binding free energy are pre-organised, low-B anchors, not floppy high-B
loops --- and the classic flexibility--antigenicity correlation concerns where antibodies \emph{bind}
(surface loops), not which residue carries the binding \emph{energy}, exactly the
proximity-vs-energetics distinction of \S\ref{sec:motivation}.

\paragraph{Flexibility versus energetic isolation.} That the hotspots are rigid rather than mobile is
less a contradiction of the flexibility hypothesis than a pointer to a different variable. Westhof's
mobility~\cite{westhof1984} and our B-factor test both ask about \emph{motion}; but the property that
makes a surface patch easy for an antibody to engage may instead be \emph{energetic and dynamic
isolation} --- the thesis of Serapian and Colombo~\cite{serapian2020}, whose MD-based energy
decomposition (a matrix of local inter-residue coupling energies) identifies antibody epitopes as
surface regions \emph{weakly coupled} to the rest of the protein: substructures that make few
stabilising nonbonded contacts with the core, sit near energetic frustration, and behave as
semi-autonomous dynamic units. Such a patch can be grabbed without fighting the protein's own
stability network --- and if it is also locally rigid (pre-organised), the antibody pays little
conformational-entropy penalty to hold it: \emph{decoupled enough to grab, ordered enough to keep}.
On this view the predictive quantity is not raw mobility but a residue's \emph{coupling} to its
environment --- intra-protein nonbonded interaction energy and the correlation structure of its
motions. Those are exactly the features the apo-MD program targets (intra-protein energetics,
correlated motion, FRESEAN mode participation), and they are blind spots of both static baselines
here, which see exposure and motion but not coupling.

\paragraph{Where this leaves us.} Neither static proxy recovers the energetic hotspots from a single
crystal: exposure has no precision ($\sim 3\%$), and mobility points the wrong way ($\rho = -0.29$).
That is precisely the opening for apo-MD --- RMSF is a cleaner, crystal-contact-free mobility measure,
and the trajectory additionally exposes the correlated-motion, mode-participation (FRESEAN), and
hydration (3D-2PT) features that no single structure carries. The question the rest of this work
asks is whether dynamics \emph{done properly} recovers signal where these static proxies fail.
The B-factor is a reasonable first-pass mobility proxy --- it is the standard one --- though an
imperfect measure of intrinsic flexibility: crystal contacts, resolution, and refinement choices
all contribute, and the per-structure z-score removes only the overall scale. MD RMSF measures the
same quantity for the free protein without those confounds, and is the cleaner test to come.
